## Summary

This study compared two instructional arrangements for foreign-language tact acquisition: covert repetition instruction and a vocal blocking procedure. Ten undergraduate students learned Spanish tacts using an adapted alternating treatments design.

Across most participants, fewer sessions were required to reach mastery under the covert repetition instruction condition than under the vocal blocking condition. Maintenance of acquired tacts also tended to be higher following covert repetition instruction, although interpretation is constrained by differential post-mastery exposure across conditions. The study did not directly measure covert rehearsal; therefore, the findings are best interpreted in terms of how different instructional arrangements structured observable response requirements during learning.
